How to Get a 99+ ATAR: A Complete Study Guide

Achieving a 99+ ATAR requires more than just hard work—it needs strategic planning and efficient study techniques. Here's what worked for me and my students.

1. Start Early and Stay Consistent

Year 12 is a marathon, not a sprint. Start revising from Day 1 of Term 1. Cramming might work for a test, but ATAR exams require long-term retention.

2. Understand, Don't Memorize

Deep understanding of concepts is far more valuable than rote memorization. When you truly understand the "why" behind equations and reactions, you can apply knowledge to unfamiliar questions.

3. Practice Past Exams

  • Complete at least 5 past papers per subject
  • Time yourself under exam conditions
  • Review answers carefully—understand why you got something wrong
  • Identify patterns in questions that repeat

4. Active Recall & Spaced Repetition

Instead of re-reading notes, test yourself. Use flashcards, summarize topics from memory, and review material at increasing intervals.

5. Take Care of Your Mental Health

Burnout is real. Sleep 8+ hours, exercise regularly, and take breaks. A healthy mind performs better than an exhausted one.

SACE Mathematical Methods: Exam Strategies That Work

Mathematical Methods is one of the most challenging SACE subjects. Here's how to maximize your score:

Key Topics to Master

  • Calculus: Differentiation rules, integration techniques, applications (optimization, rates of change)
  • Functions: Graphing, transformations, domain/range

Exam Tips

Show all working—examiners award marks for method even if final answer is wrong. For challenging questions, break them into smaller parts you can solve.

How to Ace Your Medicine Interview: MMI Preparation Guide

The Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) is designed to assess communication skills, ethical reasoning, and suitability for medicine. Here's how to prepare:

Common MMI Stations

  • Ethical Scenarios: Discuss dilemmas like resource allocation, patient autonomy
  • Motivation: Why medicine? Why this university?
  • Current Health Issues: Stay updated on Australian healthcare topics
  • Communication: Explain complex medical concepts to a patient

Preparation Tips

Practice out loud with friends or family. Record yourself and review. Focus on structure: State your stance, provide reasoning, consider counterarguments, conclude.

SACE Physics: Master Mechanics and Electromagnetism

Physics demands both conceptual understanding and problem-solving skills. Here's how to ace it:

Key Areas to Master

  • Mechanics: Newton's laws, projectile motion, energy conservation
  • Electromagnetism: Circuits, magnetic fields, electromagnetic induction
  • Waves: Wave properties, interference, optics

Problem-Solving Strategy

Always start by identifying what you're solving for. List given information, select relevant formulas, and show all working. Draw diagrams—they help visualize the problem.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Don't skip units. Don't assume direction. Always check if your answer makes physical sense.

SACE English: Essay Writing That Scores

English requires analysis, not summary. Here's how to write essays that impress:

Thesis Statement

Your essay needs a clear, arguable thesis. Avoid statements of fact—take a stance and defend it throughout.

PEEL Paragraphs

  • Point: State your argument
  • Evidence: Quote from the text
  • Explanation: Analyse how the evidence supports your point
  • Link: Connect back to your thesis

Language Analysis

Don't just identify techniques—explain their effect. Why did the author use that metaphor? How does it make the reader feel?

Exam Day: How to Perform Under Pressure

What you do on exam day matters as much as your preparation. Here's how to ace it:

Before the Exam

  • Sleep: Aim for 8 hours—cramming destroys retention
  • Eat: Breakfast with protein and complex carbs
  • Arrive early: Find your seat, settle in

During the Exam

  • Read all questions first: Identify your strongest areas
  • Time allocation: Spend time proportional to marks
  • Answer easy questions first: Build confidence
  • Show working: You can get partial marks

If You Panic

Take 3 deep breaths. Read the question again slowly. Even if you don't know the answer, write something—blank paper gets zero marks.

UCAT Preparation: Strategies for Each Section

The University Clinical Aptitude Test (UCAT) is a critical component of medicine admissions. Here's how to ace each section:

Verbal Reasoning

Read actively—don't skim. Underline key points. Practice extracting main ideas from dense passages. Time is tight: 21 minutes for 44 questions.

Decision Making

Learn to identify logical fallacies. Practice Venn diagrams and probability questions. Some questions have multiple correct answers—choose the best one.

Quantitative Reasoning

Master your calculator—speed matters. Know percentages, ratios, and data interpretation cold. Practice interpreting charts and graphs quickly.

Abstract Reasoning

Look for patterns in shape, colour, and position. Learn the common question types: Set A vs Set B, complete the series. Pattern recognition improves with practice.

Situational Judgment

This tests your people skills. Prioritise patient safety, follow protocols, and demonstrate empathy. There's often an obvious "best" answer—trust your instincts.

Dealing with Year 12 Exam Anxiety

It's normal to feel anxious about exams—but it shouldn't control you. Here's how to manage exam anxiety:

Recognise the Symptoms

  • Racing thoughts or blank mind
  • Physical symptoms: sweaty palms, rapid heartbeat
  • Procrastination or avoidance

Practical Strategies

  • Preparation reduces anxiety: The more you prepare, the more confident you'll feel
  • Practice under exam conditions: Simulate the real thing to desensitise yourself
  • Breathing exercises: 4-7-8 breathing (inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s)
  • Exercise regularly: Even 20 minutes of walking helps reduce anxiety

On Exam Day

Eat breakfast, arrive early, and remember: one exam doesn't define you. Do your best, then move on.

Year 11 Subject Selection: A Strategic Guide

Choosing Year 11 subjects sets the foundation for Year 12 and your ATAR. Here's how to choose wisely:

Consider Your Strengths

Pick subjects where you've historically performed well. A good grade in a "easier" subject beats a poor grade in a "hard" one.

Think About Prerequisites

If you want to study medicine, check university prerequisites. Many require Chemistry and often Math Methods or Biology. Plan backwards from your goals.

Balance Your Load

Don't load up on all the hardest subjects at once. Mix challenging subjects with ones you're confident in. Year 11 is about building skills, not suffering.

Talk to Seniors

Ask Year 12 students about their experience. Which teachers are helpful? Which subjects are more work than they seem?

A Parent's Guide to Supporting Year 12 Students

Your support matters—but sometimes it's hard to know what helps. Here's how parents can truly support their Year 12 student:

What to Say

  • "I'm proud of the effort you're putting in"
  • "How can I help make things easier?"
  • "It's okay to feel stressed—let's take a break together"

What Not to Say

  • "You need to study more" (they know)
  • "I was under pressure too" (dismisses their experience)
  • "What did you get in that test?" (focuses on results, not effort)

Practical Support

  • Create a quiet study environment
  • Ensure they eat well and sleep enough
  • Help with chores so they can focus on study
  • Drive them to tutoring or exam prep sessions

When to Worry

If your child shows signs of depression, severe anxiety, or self-harm, seek professional help immediately. Academic success isn't worth their mental health.

Staying Motivated Throughout Year 12

Year 12 is a marathon. Here's how to maintain motivation from Term 1 to final exams:

Set Milestones, Not Just Goals

"Get 90% in Methods" is vague. "Complete Chapter 3 exercises by Thursday" is specific and achievable.

Celebrate Small Wins

Finished a practice exam? Solved a hard problem? That's worth acknowledging. Small celebrations maintain momentum.

Visualise the End Goal

Why are you doing this? University admission? Your dream career? Keep that vision in mind when motivation fades.

Find Your People

Study groups, tutoring, or just a mate who's also grinding—social accountability helps. You don't have to do this alone.

Rest Is Productive

Taking breaks isn't laziness—it's necessary. Your brain needs downtime to consolidate learning.
